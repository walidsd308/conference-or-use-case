\section{Related Work}

Operating room scheduling has been extensively studied across multiple domains \cite{AlAmin2024}, with various techniques being applied to address its inherent complexity. Among heuristic approaches, Kamran et al. \cite{Kamran2020} applied a column-generation-heuristic-based Benders' decomposition on adaptive allocation scheduling of patients in operating rooms, while Wang et al. \cite{Wang2024} developed a Wasserstein distributionally robust optimization model with a rolling horizon rescheduling scheme for surgery scheduling involving both elective and emergency patients.
In the field of artificial intelligence, recent studies have leveraged reinforcement learning and machine learning techniques to tackle scheduling challenges. Lex et al. \cite{Lex2025} applied a two-stage predict-then-optimize approach combining machine learning prediction with scheduling optimization for elective orthopedic surgery. Similarly, Zhao et al. \cite{Zhao2025} introduced a hybrid framework integrating multi-level optimization with reinforcement learning and column generation for operating room scheduling. El-Balka et al. \cite{ElBalka2025} proposed a dynamic operation room scheduling (DORS) strategy combining explainable AI for feature selection with a fuzzy interface engine for intraday surgical scheduling. Furthermore, Eshghali et al. \cite{Eshghali2024} applied a random forest machine learning model combined with genetic algorithm and particle swarm optimization for integrated scheduling and rescheduling of elective and emergency patients.
Several studies have also adopted stochastic approaches to handle inherent uncertainties in surgical scheduling \cite{Bernardelli2024}. M'Hallah and Visintin \cite{MHallah2019} applied a stochastic integer programming model with sample average approximation for scheduling elective surgeries in a cyclic Master Surgical Schedule, whereas Zhang et al. \cite{Zhang2021} developed a two-phase optimization model combining Markov decision process and stochastic programming for advance surgery scheduling.
Exact solution methods have also been widely explored in this domain. Çelik et al. \cite{Celik2023} applied a two-stage stochastic mixed-integer programming model with a progressive hedging algorithm for surgery scheduling under the parallel processing principle. Schneider et al. \cite{Schneider2020} employed a MILP approach for scheduling surgery groups while considering multiple downstream resources. Miao and Wang \cite{Miao2021} proposed a MIP model with a preventive-reactive scheduling procedure for elective and emergency surgery scheduling with waiting time limits. Akbarzadeh and Maenhout \cite{Akbarzadeh2025} developed a dedicated branch-price-and-cut algorithm for simultaneous patient planning and surgeon scheduling in operating rooms. Additionally, Makboul et al. \cite{Makboul2025} applied a multiobjective $\epsilon$-constraint based robust optimization approach to the master surgical schedule problem under multiple uncertainties, including surgery duration and downstream resource constraints.
Other relevant studies have further contributed to this growing body of literature \cite{Nash2025, IntegratedRobust2025}.

This study is directly relevant, Spratt and Kozan \cite{Spratt2019} proposed constructive heuristics with real-time reactive strategies for the multiple operating room surgical case sequencing problem. Additionally, this heuristic approach achieves real-time performance. In this paper \cite{IntegratedRobust2025}, the authors have applied a disruption management model based on distributionally robust optimization and machine learning consensus on integrated scheduling for inpatient surgery and ambulatory surgery; they found that their approach effectively coordinates shared surgeons across different operating rooms while handling uncertainty in surgery duration and disruptions.


In this study, we focus on two main objectives. The first is to propose an optimized schedule that minimizes operational costs and maximizes block utilization while respecting all hard constraints. The second is to prioritize patient choice and flexibility by proposing two to three distinct feasible slots per patient, taking into consideration all patient availabilities and guaranteeing at least one feasible slot for every patient.
