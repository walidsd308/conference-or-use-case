

\section{Introduction}
Operating room scheduling is a research domain that continues to attract significant interest, with ongoing novelty in methodological approaches and practical applications. It is classified as a multi-objective optimization problem. The objectives are first to minimize costs: in this study \cite{10.1001/jamasurg.2017.6233} surgical cate accounts for nearly one third of US health care spending. And second, to maximize operational utilization, considering the hospital's operational constraints and operation types, while also accounting for the comfort of medical staff and patients. Most studies focus on operational aspects and propose deterministic and stochastic optimization models, which generate a predetermined schedule without taking patient choice into account. This is understandable for certain types of operations, such as cardiovascular or emergency cases, where the intervention should be performed as soon as possible.

However, this study is set in the orthopedic department at the Centre Hospitalier Universitaire (CHU) Montpellier (France), which provides a suitable environment for developing a patient-centered scheduling approach. Indeed, unlike emergency or time-critical surgical cases, elective orthopedic patients generally have more flexibility in their scheduling preferences, even when urgent cases are involved. This flexibility enables the integration of patient preferences into scheduling decisions. This work aims to balance operational efficiency with patient care, enabling patients to actively participate in the scheduling process.

As shown in Figure \ref{fig:methodology_flow}, this approach is based on an iterative optimization methodology. First, patient and physician data are uploaded into the system. Based on this dataset, the MILP model generates up to three slots that match the availability of both patients and physicians. Next, the flexibility-first heuristic identifies the patient with the most flexible schedule, as determined by the quality scores $\sigma_p$ (detailed in Subsection \ref{sec:patient_ordering}). The selected patient reviews the available options and chooses the one that works best for them. Then, the unused options are redistributed to the remaining patients, and the system recalculates which patient to process next until there are no patients left to assign. By actively including the patients in the scheduling system, this iterative method combines both efficiency and care.
