

\section{Introduction}
Operating room scheduling is a multi-objective optimization problem \cite{AlAmin2024}. The objectives are to minimize costs and maximize operational utilization under hospital constraints while accounting for staff and patient needs. For instance, surgical care accounts for nearly one-third of U.S. healthcare spending \cite{jamasurg}. Most studies propose deterministic or stochastic models that generate a predetermined schedule without patient choice \cite{Kamran2020, Zhang2021, Celik2023}. This is reasonable for time-critical cases such as cardiovascular or emergency surgery.

However, this study is set in the orthopedic department at the Centre Hospitalier Universitaire (CHU) Montpellier (France), where a patient-centered scheduling approach is practical. Indeed, unlike emergency or time-critical surgical cases, elective orthopedic patients generally have a more flexible schedule, even when urgent cases are involved. This flexibility allows patient preferences to be included in scheduling. This work balances operational efficiency with patient care by letting them participate in scheduling.

As shown in Figure \ref{fig:methodology_flow}, this approach is based on an iterative optimization methodology. First, patient and physician data are uploaded into the system. Based on this dataset, the MILP model generates up to three slots that match the availability of both patients and physicians. Next, the flexibility-first heuristic identifies the patient with the most flexible schedule, as determined by the quality scores $\sigma_p$ (detailed in Subsection \ref{sec:patient_ordering}). The selected patient reviews the available options and chooses the one that works best for them. Then, the unused options are redistributed to the remaining patients, and the system recalculates which patient to process next until there are no patients left to assign. This iterative method includes patients in the scheduling loop while preserving efficiency.
